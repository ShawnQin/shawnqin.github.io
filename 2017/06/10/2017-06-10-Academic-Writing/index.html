Academic writing

Posted on 2017-06-10 | Post modified: 2019-04-01 | In academic | Visitors:
Words count in article: 1.4k | Reading time ≈ 8
resource on improving academical writing

Writing your first paper can be daunting and scaring, especially for non native English speakers and without any training on academic writing. After years of struggling with your projects, you are ready to write a journal article to wrap all your exciting scientific discoveries. You set a goal and a time table aiming to finish your paper in two weeks. Every morning, you seat down, open your MS Word and start to write. You find it hard to get the words you want pop out and your mind eventually drifts away to other stuffs. After one hour, you find you could hardly write only a single paragraph. As the deadline approaches, you feel anxious and depressed. If you ever experience this kind of emotion, you are not alone. Even for native English speakers, writing academic paper is not an easy task. Writing paper is the central part of research, as George Whitesides put it “ ‘Interesting and unpublished’ is equivalent to ‘non-existent’ “. In this post, I will list all the resource and suggestions I got during my study in graduate school, hopefully, this will relieve some of the pain in writing your first paper.

Some suggestions

Academic writing differs a lot from other forms of writing. Here I summarize some online resources that I found very helpful when writing my first journal-like paper. While the source mainly focus on the field of biomedicine, it should also applicable to other STEM and even humanity disciplines.

To improve writing quality, practice is essential. Rewriting and revision are also important. Only after several round of iterations between writing and revision, could a draft look like fulfill all the requirement for publication. Even good writers take rewrite seriously. What I learned is that when doing your first draft, you should just write and do not bother all the errors such as grammars and typos. The important thing is letting your though pour out without interruption.

The general idea or principle of revision is using a “top-down” approach. A good revision strategy is starting from the outline.The first draft often looks awful and requires major revision. Revising your outline help you reorganize your thoughts in a more coherent and logical way. Don’t hesitate if you need delete some paragraphs in your first draft. Keep it in mind, less is more. The outline

To save time, one tip I have learned is that organize the outline and structure and discuss with others, such as your supervisor and colleagues. This can be done in various tools such as Xmind. One of the prominent scientist, George Whitesides, point it out “A good outline for the paper is also a good plan for the research program. You should write and rewrite these plans/outlines throughout the course of the research”.

The practical advice I received on writing an outline is doing “brainstorm”. First, you write down all the idea, items, phrases that pop out from your mind. Then, you categorize that according to the categories. For example, some of them may belong to the “motivation” of your project. Some of them may fall into the “discussion” part, others may be grouped as “background” or “introduction”.

Regular writing time

The best practice to improving academic writing is find a regular time slot to write every day (or at least weekdays) and stick to it. You should make sure during that time block you will not be interrupted by other things. For me, I find it early in morning is an idea time for writing. Others may prefer the midnight. Writing is a highly intensive labor and demands a lot of energy and focus. To release the bitter, half an hour to one hour is enough everyday. At the beginning of your project, you might find there is not so much progress to write every day. You can write whatever you want, such as the summary of a talk in listen today, the interest thing you experienced, or even keep a diary. The key is sticking to it and foster this habit. After one month, you will feel more comfortable in writing in English.

Take myself as an example. Although I have heard this advice from Prof. Bruce at the second year of my PhD study. I made mind to keep writing everyday for 10 mins, but failed after two weeks. But the second time when I decide to keep keep writing everyday was 2016, when I went back from UC Berkeley. I realized that I have to try my best to improve my English, including speaking and writing. To force my self to write, I volunteered to write a book on quantitative biology together with Chao. The writing process was so painful but also fruitful, at least it greatly improved my English writing skill. Had I know the difficulties, I would probably not volunteered to do it. The lesson I learned during writing that book is that no matter how hard it is, if you can stick to the time table, write regularly everyday, you will astonished by your own achievement. It really large strengthen my confidence of writing. As a comparison, it took me about one month to write my first paper in the early 2016, while I finished my first draft of my second paper in just one week last summer.

Useful source on academic writing

Here I list several useful online writing source and books about academic writing

  • How to write your first research paper. If I have to recommend only one short paper on writing, that’s it. This is wrote by an advisor in the center of career development at Cornell. It is well written and vividly express the emotional struggling we experienced when writing our first paper.

  • How to write a paper in scientific journal stytle and format. This online source from Bates College provide comprehensive and doable tutorial on writing journal style paper in biomedical field. It is not very long and provides many practical advices in writing journal articles.

  • Whitesides’ Group: Writing a paper. Suggestions on writing outline from a prominent scientist in Harvard. I could not agree more on his opinion. The process of crafting the outline is essentially a practice to condensate the idea and most important finding of your project. I provides a logical flow needed when you write which is from my point of view the most important ingredient of a good article.

  • English Communication for Scientists. A column from Nature education, provides general guide principle not only on academic writing but also communication skills to general audience.

  • How to write research articles. From UCSF career center, you can also find useful concise tips on delivering scientific presentation, designing a poster and job interview.

  • Resource from UIUC, gives very comprehensive tips, from style to paragraph to grammar.

Some books covering this topic

  • Science research writing for non-native speakers of English. By Hilary Glasman-Deal.I found this book very handy and friendly. It not only tell you the general principles of academic writing, but it also tell you how to do it from scratch, step by step. Just as learning any other skills, what we need to do is practice from the basic build blocks. After familiarize with the structure, the paragraphs, the sentences and the words, you will feel more confident to writing
  • To the point — a dictionary of concise writing. By Robert Hartwell Fiske. I have not finish reading it, but it has a very good reputation.
  • A manual for writers of research papers, theses and dissertations. 7th edition.
  • The elements of stytle. By William Strunk Jr. This is perhaps the most famous book on writing
  • On writing well - the classic guide to writing nonfiction. By William Zinsser.
  • The craft of research. By Wayne Booth, Greg Colomb, and Joe Williams
  • The Scientist’s Guide to Writing: How to Write More Easily and Effectively throughout Your Scientific Career.
  • Academic writing for publish — A practical handbook.
  • A field guide for science writers.
  • Style— Toward clarity and grace.
  • Writing science — how to write papers that get cited and proposals that get funded
  • The sense of style. by Steven Pinker.
  • The Chicago manual of style. Very large book and authoritative reference book in English writing.
